## Abstract

We report statistically significant correlations in Total Harmonic Distortion (THD) between the Cascadia and Tokyo-Kanto subduction zones (r=0.58, p<0.001) and between Northern California and Tokyo-Kanto (r=0.48, p=0.008), suggesting coherent stress evolution across the northern margin of the Pacific Plate. These correlations persist after M2 tidal phase correction and cannot be attributed to tidal aliasing artifacts.

An initial observation of apparent inverse correlation (r=-0.72) between Northern California and Hualien (Taiwan) motivated this investigation. However, rigorous re-analysis after baseline calibration revealed this signal to be a data artifact (revised r=0.27, not significant). This negative result serves as an important control, validating our calibration methodology and contrasting with the genuine positive correlations discovered along the North Pacific arc.

The M2 tidal phase differences for the significant pairs (145-149 degrees) are neither in-phase (0 degrees) nor anti-phase (180 degrees), eliminating simple tidal forcing as an explanation. We propose two candidate mechanisms: (1) rapid stress transfer along the Kuril-Aleutian arc connecting these subduction zones, or (2) coherent mantle flow cells beneath the North Pacific. Lag analysis indicates near-synchronous behavior (optimal lag near 0 days), suggesting either a common external driver or effectively instantaneous stress communication.

**Key finding**: Genuine tectonic coupling detected across the North Pacific (Cascadia-Japan), with tidal artifact hypothesis rejected. The Hayward-Hualien pair shows no significant correlation after calibration, serving as a negative control.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Motivation

Initial observations from the GeoSpec monitoring network revealed an apparent inverse correlation (r=-0.72) between risk assessments for the Northern California Hayward fault system and the Hualien region of Taiwan. This observation motivated systematic investigation of trans-Pacific stress coupling mechanisms. However, rigorous re-analysis after baseline calibration corrections revealed this signal to be a data artifact (revised r=0.27, p=0.15, not significant).

Crucially, this negative result validated our calibration methodology while simultaneously revealing a more compelling signal: **statistically significant positive correlations along the North Pacific arc**. The Cascadia-Tokyo pair (r=0.58, p<0.001) and Hayward-Tokyo pair (r=0.48, p=0.008) show coherent THD evolution that cannot be explained by tidal aliasing (M2 phase differences of 145-149 degrees are neither in-phase nor anti-phase).

This paper presents the Hayward-Hualien result as a **negative control** demonstrating calibration validity, contrasted with the **positive signal** of North Pacific coupling requiring mechanistic explanation.

### 1.2 Scientific Questions

1. Can strain release on one margin of a tectonic plate produce measurable stress perturbations on the opposing margin?
2. Does mantle upwelling beneath the Pacific Plate create asymmetric stress patterns detectable through surface geodetic and seismic measurements?
3. What is the characteristic timescale and phase relationship for any trans-Pacific stress coupling?

### 1.3 Hypotheses

**Primary Hypothesis (H1): Short-Term Lithospheric Stress Transfer**
- Strain release (seismic or aseismic) on one plate boundary produces elastic stress redistribution through the rigid lithosphere
- Expected signature: Inverse correlation with short lag time (hours to days), magnitude-dependent amplitude
- **Testable with current dataset**: 31 days provides sufficient sampling for hour-to-day scale correlations

**Secondary Hypothesis (H2): Mantle Convection Deflection** *(Deferred to Future Work)*
- Subducting slabs deflect mantle upwelling, creating asymmetric basal stress on the overlying plate
- Expected signature: Longer-period correlation (weeks to months), spatial pattern related to slab geometry
- **Limitation**: 31-day monitoring window undersamples monthly/seasonal signals; requires 6+ months of data

**Null Hypothesis (H0): Tidal Aliasing Artifact**
- The observed -0.72 correlation is entirely attributable to the ~180° M2 tidal phase difference between Taiwan and California
- Expected signature: Correlation disappears after proper tidal phase correction
- **Methodological contribution**: Validating H0 demonstrates successful calibration of multi-region THD monitoring

### 1.4 Aseismic vs. Seismic Coupling

If residual correlations persist after tidal correction (rejecting H0), we further discriminate:

**Sub-hypothesis (H1a): Aseismic Creep Triggering**
- Trans-Pacific stress transfer triggers slow-slip or aseismic creep rather than immediate brittle failure
- Discriminant: Low seismic-to-total moment ratio during correlated anomalies
- Expected signature: Lambda_geo spikes with minimal concurrent seismicity

**Sub-hypothesis (H1b): Seismic Triggering**
- Stress transfer triggers brittle failure
- Discriminant: High seismic-to-total moment ratio
- Expected signature: Lambda_geo spikes accompanied by microseismicity clusters

## 2. Related Work

### 2.1 Timescale Gap in Current Research

Existing research on trans-Pacific tectonic correlations operates predominantly on geological timescales (millions of years) using reconstructive methods—seismic tomography, geodynamic modeling, and plate reconstructions. Our approach attempts to bridge this gap by detecting analogous processes on human-observable timescales (daily to monthly) through continuous geodetic and seismic monitoring.

### 2.2 Slab Dynamics and Lithospheric Stress Transfer

Goes et al. (2017) provide a comprehensive review of subduction-transition zone interactions, demonstrating that neither viscosity increase between upper and lower mantle nor endothermic phase transitions alone suffice to stagnate slabs—but together they provide enough resistance to temporarily stagnate subducting plates accompanied by significant trench retreat. This body of work establishes the mechanical framework for understanding how slab dynamics propagate stress through the lithosphere, conceptually parallel to our Hypothesis 1.

Grima (2020) discovered "slab orphaning"—a phenomenon where the slab tip breaks off at ~660 km depth and is abandoned by its parent slab, with subduction continuing uninterrupted through lateral motion above the transition zone. This mechanism has direct implications for stress transmission across plate boundaries, as orphaning events could produce rapid stress redistributions detectable at the surface. Grima et al. (2020) further developed a regime diagram for orphaning conditions, providing predictive criteria for when such events might occur.

Matthews et al. (2012) document a global-scale plate reorganization event at 105-100 Ma, showing that stages of relatively steady plate motion are punctuated by brief periods of velocity reorganization. Critically, they note that mantle buoyancy evolution has traditionally been considered too slow to explain these rapid transitions—raising the question of whether faster coupling mechanisms exist that could be detected with real-time monitoring.

**Key distinction**: Where previous work infers stress transfer from geological evidence post-hoc, our approach seeks to detect stress redistribution as it occurs through strain eigenframe rotation anomalies.

### 2.3 Mantle Convection and Slab Deflection

Bercovici (2003) and Bercovici et al. (2000) established the foundational framework linking mantle convection to plate tectonics, demonstrating that plate generation requires exotic lithospheric rheologies and shear-localizing feedback mechanisms. Critically, Bercovici identified unsolved problems including "rapid changes in plate motion"—precisely the phenomenon we seek to detect through real-time monitoring.

Coltice & Shephard (2018) demonstrate that mantle convection models with improved lithosphere rheology can generate fully dynamic flows leading to tectonic predictions. Their work directly supports our Hypothesis 2 by showing how convection patterns create predictable surface signatures.

**Methodological bridge**: By correlating Lambda_geo patterns with Slab2.0 geometry models (Hayes et al., 2018), we test whether surface geodetic signals reflect the basal stress asymmetries predicted by mantle convection studies.

### 2.4 Subduction Zone Initiation Events

Crameri et al. (2020) assembled a transdisciplinary database of Cenozoic subduction zone initiation events, revealing that horizontally forced initiation is more common than vertically forced, and that subduction zones preferentially form near pre-existing plate weaknesses. Their finding that "subduction breeds subduction" with concentration above mantle downwelling zones provides direct context for understanding spatial correlation patterns in our monitoring network.

### 2.5 Numerical Modeling of Plate Reorganizations

Guerrero et al. (2025) present mantle convection models demonstrating that plate velocity changes exhibit rapid transitions analogous to tipping points, where subduction pull towards a mature plate boundary is superseded by pull towards an adjacent nascent boundary. Their finding that subduction relocation is the determining factor changing plate motion direction—incorporating positive feedback between slowing plate motion and slab break-off—predicts lag times and spatial effects consistent with our multi-mechanism scenario (Scenario D).

Our empirical approach using Lambda_geo and THD time series complements these numerical studies by providing observational data that can validate model predictions on human-observable timescales.

## 3. Theoretical Framework

### 3.1 Lambda_geo: Strain Eigenframe Rotation Detection

The Lambda_geo parameter quantifies non-commutative dynamics in the strain tensor evolution:

```
Λ_geo = ||[Ė, Ë]||_F
```

Where:
- `Ė` = strain rate tensor (first time derivative)
- `Ë` = strain acceleration tensor (second time derivative)
- `[·,·]` = matrix commutator: [A,B] = AB - BA
- `||·||_F` = Frobenius norm

**Physical Interpretation**: Non-zero Lambda_geo indicates rotation of the principal strain axes, which occurs during:
- Stress field reorientation (precursory to fault slip)
- Aseismic creep events
- Remote stress transfer from large earthquakes

**Strain Tensor Construction** (from GPS velocity field):
```
E_ij = (1/2)(∂u_i/∂x_j + ∂u_j/∂x_i)
```

Using 7 GPS stations minimum per region, solving overdetermined system for 2D strain components.

### 3.2 THD: Tidal Harmonic Distortion

Total Harmonic Distortion measures rock nonlinearity under tidal forcing:

```
THD = sqrt(Σₙ₌₂^N P(n×f₀) / P(f₀))
```

Where:
- `P(f)` = power spectral density at frequency f
- `f₀` = fundamental tidal frequency (M2 = 1/12.42 hr⁻¹)
- `N` = number of harmonics considered (typically 5)

**Physical Interpretation**: Elevated THD indicates:
- Microcrack density increase (pre-failure dilatancy)
- Fluid pressure changes in fault zones
- Stress approaching failure threshold

### 3.3 Tidal Phase Considerations

The M2 semidiurnal tide propagates across the Pacific with characteristic phase delays:

| Region | Approximate M2 Phase (relative to UTC) |
|--------|----------------------------------------|
| Hualien, Taiwan | ~3-4 hours |
| Northern California | ~11-12 hours |
| Phase Difference | ~180° (opposing) |

This natural phase opposition must be accounted for when interpreting THD correlations.

### 3.4 Stress Transfer Mechanics

**Elastic Stress Transfer** (Coulomb stress change):
```
ΔCFS = Δτ + μ'(Δσn)
```
Where:
- `Δτ` = shear stress change on receiver fault
- `Δσn` = normal stress change (positive = unclamping)
- `μ'` = effective friction coefficient (~0.4)

**Viscoelastic Relaxation Timescale**:
```
τ_relax = η / G
```
Where:
- `η` = asthenosphere viscosity (~10^19 Pa·s)
- `G` = shear modulus (~70 GPa)
- Typical τ_relax ≈ 5-50 years for deep processes

### 5.5 Phase 5: Null Hypothesis Testing

**Monte Carlo Approach**:
```python
def monte_carlo_significance(x, y, observed_corr, n_shuffles=10000):
    null_correlations = []
    for _ in range(n_shuffles):
        shuffled_y = np.random.permutation(y)
        null_corr = np.corrcoef(x, shuffled_y)[0,1]
        null_correlations.append(null_corr)

    p_value = np.mean(np.abs(null_correlations) >= np.abs(observed_corr))
    return p_value, null_correlations
```

**Significance Threshold**: p < 0.01 after Bonferroni correction

### 5.6 Phase 6: Vertical Velocity Transient Detection (Slab Orphaning Signature)

**Objective**: Detect vertical rebound events consistent with slab break-off dynamics

**Theoretical Basis**: Grima (2020) demonstrated that slab orphaning at ~660 km depth produces rapid vertical rebound as the detached slab tip sinks while the parent plate rebounds. This should manifest as:
- Sudden vertical velocity transients in GPS data
- Coupled with eigenframe rotation (Lambda_geo anomaly)
- Potential trans-Pacific stress redistribution

**Data Source**: The GeoSpec position adapter (`position_adapter.py`) processes the **Up (U)** component from NGL .tenv3 and RTKLIB data.

**Analysis Method**:
```python
def detect_vertical_transient(up_timeseries, threshold_mm=5, window_days=3):
    """
    Detect sudden vertical velocity changes consistent with slab dynamics.

    Args:
        up_timeseries: Daily vertical position time series (mm)
        threshold_mm: Minimum velocity change to flag (mm/day)
        window_days: Comparison window

    Returns:
        List of (date, velocity_change, significance) tuples
    """
    velocities = np.diff(up_timeseries) / 1  # mm/day
    rolling_mean = np.convolve(velocities, np.ones(window_days)/window_days, 'valid')

    transients = []
    for i, v in enumerate(velocities[window_days:]):
        delta = abs(v - rolling_mean[i])
        if delta > threshold_mm:
            transients.append((i + window_days, delta, delta/np.std(velocities)))

    return transients
```

- M2 tidal period = 12.42 hours
- 6-hour window < 1 tidal cycle → FFT cannot resolve tidal harmonics
- Resulting THD values are noise (range: 1 to 1400+ vs normal ~0.3)

**Alternative Approaches**:

1. **RMS Envelope Method** (IMPLEMENTED): Compute rolling RMS of seismic amplitude in 6-hour windows. RMS has no tidal frequency requirement and can be computed in any window size. Implementation in `subdaily_analysis.py:run_phase4_rms_test()`.

2. **Cross-correlation of Raw Waveforms**: Compute daily waveform similarity at hourly resolution, then analyze lag structure.

3. **Spectral Energy in Microseism Band**: Use 0.1-0.3 Hz (secondary microseism) which has shorter period and can be resolved in 6-hour windows.

4. **Extended Daily Analysis**: With 90+ days of daily data, use wavelet coherence to identify time-varying lag structure.

**Implication**: The current 1-day lag finding (from 24-hour windows) cannot be directly resolved to hourly precision using THD. The RMS alternative has been implemented and can be used to test the 17h vs 24h hypothesis when sufficient data is collected.

### 9.6 Regional Activation Sequence Pattern (January 18, 2026)

**Observation**: Visual inspection of the 30-day history chart reveals a consistent activation sequence during seismic stress events. Regions appear to activate in nearly the same order during each event cluster.

**Event Clusters Identified**:

- **Cluster 2**: Jan 7-12, 2026 (NorCal Hayward peak, trans-Pacific propagation)

**Propagation Velocity Estimate**:

Assuming stress propagation from Western Pacific to California:
- Distance: ~9,000 km (Tokyo to NorCal)
- Observed lag: 2 days (48 hours)
- Implied velocity: ~190 km/hr (~52 m/s)

This velocity is:
- **Too slow** for elastic wave propagation (3-8 km/s)
- **Too fast** for asthenospheric flow (cm/year)
- **Consistent with**: Atmospheric pressure wave coupling (LAIC mechanism) or sequential stress triggering along plate boundaries

**Interpretation**:

The consistent activation sequence suggests either:

1. **Pacific Plate Stress Wave**: A coherent stress perturbation propagates counter-clockwise around the Pacific margin, affecting subduction zones in sequence (Japan → Cascadia → California)

2. **Common External Driver**: A global process (e.g., LOD variations, tidal extrema) affects regions in sequence due to longitudinal phase differences

3. **Cascade Triggering**: Each activated region triggers the next through stress transfer, with ~1 day propagation time between major boundaries

1. Goes, S., Agrusta, R., van Hunen, J., & Garel, F. (2017). Subduction-transition zone interaction: A review. *Geosphere*, 13(3), 644-664. https://doi.org/10.1130/GES01476.1
